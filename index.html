In the summer of 1973,    my father would learn the words to Tie a Yellow Ribbon     'round the Old Oak Tree, and run– his feet bare and heavy– in    12 x 12 circles on the front lawn.

He plays lotto every week-     night at the 7-11 on 347. The un-won winnings have already been thoughtfully distributed.

My father    doesn't talk about his father. ***

** Here, with his brother, Rich.      Outside his apartment, in Queens.

There are 30,000 miles on his car between Suffolk     and Nassau.

*** Challah instead of rye.

We went out a few weekends ago. I'd taken him out to breakfast, in the hopes of    buttering him up over    coffee and toast. ***

*** I'd meant to take him somewhere far nicer,    with something far nicer. ***

I ask my father if he remembers    when this was taken. He picks his cuticles in time with the wall-clock   second-hand. **

He's timid around words like   'dream' and     'lesbian.' *******

I once asked him to tell me a secret in exchange for one of my own. He did    not have anything to give.

In my mind,    he's laying on our living room carpet, sun from the window    reddening the top of his head. When he rises, he does so with a sharp exhale.

He met my mother at a furniture store. She dusted,    he stocked.

I cannot find his    father's obituary and I do not remember his middle name. ****

    There is one framed photograph of his side of the family in his house. At Christmastime,    he unsheathes the printer-paper-photo-    copy and checks for his kin on the back,    named L-to-R. *****

**** Their firsts are the same,    and same as his son,     and his son.

On my birthday, he sent me an email.     'I love you     more than you can imagine.'

   He eats peanut butter and jelly sandwiches with two napkins: one dry, for crumbs,    one wet, for stains and spills.

My father once unabashadely crawled through a playground tube    to retrieve me (weeping    and frozen on all fours– overcome by the view from so high.) I remember his starched-white shirt    against the red plastic.

He used to collect change in this horrible two-foot Budweiser-bottle piggy bank.    Now they're in cardboard boxes beneath his bed, undeposited.

   I used to sit up     on his shoulders;    rest both hands atop his head.

I ask him    to love me, and he replies.

My father thinks highly of     gothic revival architecture and all the men from    'The Curse of Oak Island.'

He mentioned once that he kept a diary when    mom started getting sick.

****** He used to pull me out of school early     to see Marvel films on release day.

My father twitches incessantly in his sleep, to the dismay     of the dog;    her head planted atop his shins    at the foot of the bed.

He rises with the sun and falls long before the moon–    though he can identify it's phases with striking accuracy.
